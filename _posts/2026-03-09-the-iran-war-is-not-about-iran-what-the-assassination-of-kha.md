On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel launched joint airstrikes across Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, members of his family, and at least thirteen senior military officials. The assassination was carried out using CIA intelligence to determine the whereabouts of Iranian leaders, with Israeli jets dropping 30 bombs on Khamenei's compound in Tehran during daylight hours. Strikes hit military and government sites across Tehran, Isfahan, Qom, Karaj, and Kermanshah. Within days, the conflict had expanded into a regional war, with Iran retaliating against Israel, US bases, and Gulf states, and the Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of the world's oil flows — effectively shutting down. On 8 March, Mojtaba Khamenei, the dead supreme leader's son, was installed as his successor, signalling continuity rather than the collapse Washington appeared to be betting on.

The mainstream framing of this event has centred on Iran's nuclear programme and the language of pre-emption. That framing is incomplete at best, and dishonest at worst. What happened last week was a calculated act of regime change, launched *during active negotiations*, with consequences that will reshape the global order for years — and the countries that will bear the heaviest costs had zero input into the decision.

## The Lie of Preventive Self-Defence

The official justification rests on a familiar scaffold: Iran posed an imminent nuclear threat, diplomacy had failed, and the strike was necessary. But the evidence directly contradicts this.

China's Foreign Minister Wang Yi told his Israeli counterpart that the attack came as negotiations between Washington and Tehran had "made significant progress, including addressing Israel's security concerns." The day before the strikes, Oman's foreign minister affirmed on US television that mediation between the US and Iran was going well and that peace was within reach. Russia's Lavrov stated that Moscow had seen no evidence Iran was developing nuclear weapons, and said the war could produce the very outcome it claimed to prevent: nuclear proliferation.

Israel's own Defence Minister disclosed that the strikes had initially been planned for mid-2026, and that Netanyahu had set the aim of assassinating Khamenei as far back as November 2025. This is not the timeline of a nation responding to an imminent threat. It is the timeline of a planned assassination. Analysts at BCA Research put it plainly: Washington and Jerusalem judged Iran was at a moment of "critical vulnerability" and "could not afford to miss the opportunity to reshape the region."

Norway's foreign minister stated the obvious: "The attack is described by Israel as a preventive strike, but it is not in line with international law. Preventive attacks require an immediately imminent threat." Senegal's Prime Minister Ousmane Sonko was sharper still: "A country, without a resolution or a mandate from the United Nations, can decide to strike other countries, to assassinate their leaders. It must be said as it is. This is extremely serious and the whole balance of the world that has been built over the last 50 years is compromised."

He is correct.

## The Strait of Hormuz: Where Rhetoric Meets Reality

Whatever one thinks of the strike's legality, its consequences are already material and global. The Strait of Hormuz has experienced ongoing disruption since 28 February, with Iran's IRGC issuing warnings prohibiting vessel passage, leading to a sharp decline in maritime transit — tanker traffic dropped first by approximately 70%, with over 150 ships anchoring outside the strait.

Helima Croft, global head of commodity strategy at RBC Capital Markets, said plainly: "We're now facing what looks like the biggest energy crisis since the oil embargo in the 1970s." The mechanism was remarkably low-tech. Iran didn't need a naval blockade — it used cheap drone strikes near the strait, and insurers and shipping companies decided it was unsafe to transit. The strait closed itself through risk calculus, not force.

The immediate beneficiary is obvious. With Middle Eastern energy production shut down or blocked from shipping, the US is now the world's largest oil exporter and the world's largest LNG producer. QatarEnergy's suspension of LNG production creates a gap which US firms like ExxonMobil and Cheniere could exploit. There is no need for conspiracy theory here — the alignment of strategic violence and commercial advantage is plain.

A particularly striking detail: on 4 March, reports emerged that Iran would allow only Chinese vessels through the strait, and on 5 March, the bulk carrier *Iron Maiden*, operated by Cetus Maritime Shanghai, transited while signalling "CHINA OWNER." The strait is not just a chokepoint for oil. It is becoming a live demonstration of whose relationships carry weight in a crisis and whose do not.

## China and Russia: Loud Words, Quiet Hands

The most revealing aspect of this crisis is what China and Russia have *not* done. Both have offered statements condemning the US, but analysts say neither is in a position to give meaningful material support beyond rhetoric.

Years of war in Ukraine have hollowed out Russia's capacity to project power beyond its borders, with its military overstretched and its economy under sustained sanctions pressure. And China's calculus is coldly pragmatic. As one analyst at the International Crisis Group put it, China "sees no benefit in heightening tension with the US over Iran" — it values its trade truce with Washington more. A weakened Iran, paradoxically, may suit Chinese interests: "The weaker the Iranian regime gets... the more diplomatically, economically and technologically dependent on China it will become."

This is the lesson of this war for anyone relying on Beijing or Moscow as a counterweight to Washington: their solidarity is conditional, calibrated, and ultimately self-serving. BRICS rhetoric about multipolarity sounds compelling in conference halls. In a shooting war, it dissolves.

## What Africa — and South Africa — Must Understand

The impact on Africa is already direct. This is not an abstract market fluctuation — it is a direct hit to household budgets and national treasuries, and the impact is deeply uneven. For net oil importers — Kenya, Zambia, Rwanda, South Africa, Ghana, and most of West Africa — the outlook is dire. Ships are rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope because Iran's Houthi allies have announced they will close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait to US and Israeli ships. South Africa gains some transit fees, but pays at the pump.

Africa's political response has been fractured. South Africa's President Ramaphosa called for restraint and adherence to international law without mentioning any involved parties by name. The ANC condemned the strikes and called for an end to US "bullying tactics." But the deeper truth — articulated by analysts at the Institute for Security Studies — is that African countries could "get trapped in the messy middle," with North and East African states facing particular exposure to proxy actors.

The structural point is this: African states are burdened by $746 billion in cumulative foreign debt, with government debt averaging around 60% of GDP, and approximately 57% of Africans live in countries that spend more on external debt servicing than on education or health care. A sustained oil price shock on top of this debt architecture is not a temporary inconvenience — it is a potential fiscal catastrophe.

The assassination of a head of state during active negotiations, using intelligence shared between allies for the explicit purpose of regime change, with full awareness that it would trigger a regional energy crisis — this is not a "strike." It is a statement about who gets to decide the shape of the world.

The United States National Security Strategy already declared openly: "The days of the United States propping up the entire world order like Atlas are over." What it did not say, but has now demonstrated, is that Washington still reserves the right to destroy any part of that order it finds inconvenient.

For South Africa, for Africa, for every country in the so-called Global South watching this, the lesson is not that you need better allies. It is that you need capabilities. Energy sovereignty. Refining capacity. Strategic reserves. Diplomatic architecture that does not depend on anyone else's goodwill. BRICS expansion may broaden the bloc's geopolitical footprint, but its institutional innovations remain parallel mechanisms rather than disruptive alternatives — a tempered form of incremental revisionism embedded within Western-dominated financial structures.
