On Saturday 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated strikes across Iran under the name "Operation Epic Fury." Iran confirmed on Sunday that Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed in the strikes. Top advisor Ali Shamkhani and IRGC commander General Mohammad Pakpour were also killed. Iranian armed forces chief Abdul Rahim Mousavi and Defence Minister Aziz Nasirzadeh were killed in the attacks as well. The stated justification was the elimination of an imminent threat. By Sunday evening, the Pentagon had admitted to Congress that this justification was false.

That admission — buried beneath the spectacle of explosions and mourning crowds — is where the real story of this war begins.

## The Lie at the Centre

The Pentagon acknowledged to congressional staff on Sunday that Iran had no plans to strike US forces or bases in the Middle East unless Israel attacked first — directly contradicting the White House's assertion that Tehran posed an imminent, preemptive threat to American personnel. The briefing, attended by bipartisan staffers from several national security committees, lasted more than 90 minutes but failed to produce clear evidence supporting the administration's stated rationale for attacking Iran.

This is not a matter of interpretation. Pentagon briefers pointed to Iran's ballistic missile program and proxy forces as justification — a threat posture that, sources noted, has existed for years. Senator Mark Warner, the vice chair of the Senate Intelligence Committee, told CNN he had seen no intelligence "that Iran was on the verge of launching any kind of preemptive strike against the United States of America," adding that Trump has "started a war of choice."

The mainstream framing over the next week will focus on operational outcomes: the dramatic killing of Khamenei, the question of Iranian succession, the tactical choreography of "Operation Epic Fury." What it will systematically avoid is the most important fact: this war was launched under a fabricated pretext. For weeks, there had been a strange split-screen: while US envoys held regular talks with Iran over a new nuclear deal, the Trump administration was amassing the largest buildup of military materiel in the Middle East since the invasion of Iraq in 2003. Although the last round of talks ended Thursday with Iran agreeing to "never" stockpile enriched uranium, that was not enough to avert US military action.

Read that again: Iran agreed to the core demand — and was attacked anyway.

## The Real Drivers

If not an imminent threat, then what? Three forces converge.

First, Netanyahu. Netanyahu has long viewed Iran as Israel's most dangerous adversary. After the fall of Syria's Assad regime and Israel's crippling of Hezbollah in Lebanon, Israel last summer launched a war against Iran itself. Although Israel halted the conflict after the US struck Iran's nuclear sites, analysts had long suspected Netanyahu would resume attacks. With elections due in October, Netanyahu may also see the return to war as a chance to shore up his standing domestically.

Second, Trump's own pattern. Trump declared that the objective of the operation is to destroy Iran's missile and military capabilities, prevent Iran from obtaining nuclear weapons, and ultimately topple the regime. This is regime change spoken aloud — the thing the US claimed for two decades it had learned not to do. A Reuters/Ipsos poll showed only 27% of Americans approved of the strikes, while 43% disapproved. The "America First" president has opened a new forever war that most Americans did not ask for.

Third, the broader posture. Geopolitical analysts warned that Saturday's strikes could be the opening salvo of a sustained military campaign aimed at dismantling the Iranian regime, with the US seeking to assert dominance over the world's most critical oil-producing region. Strip away the moral language and the architecture is visible: control of Gulf energy flows at a moment when US primacy is being challenged on every front.

## The Fallout Is Already Global

Within hours, the war spilled across borders. In retaliation, Iran launched dozens of drones and ballistic missiles throughout the Persian Gulf, targeting Israel as well as US military bases in Jordan, Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates. Closure of the Strait of Hormuz resulted in the disruption of global oil and gas shipment.

The energy market response has been immediate. Oil prices briefly touched $80 a barrel early Monday as the Iran conflict threatens to disrupt global energy supply through the Strait of Hormuz. Brent crude futures opened more than 11 percent higher at $82.37. Barclays analysts warned Brent could hit $100 per barrel. UBS said it is possible the market is looking at a material disruption that sends Brent spot prices above $120.

The Strait is not formally closed. Kpler vessel tracking shows limited traffic continuing — primarily Iranian and Chinese-flagged ships — but commercial operators, major oil companies, and insurers have effectively withdrawn from the corridor. Insurance premiums had already reached six-year highs ahead of the strikes. The result: a de facto closure for most of the global shipping community. Greece's shipping ministry has advised vessels to avoid the Persian Gulf, the Gulf of Oman and the Strait of Hormuz. Shipping giant Maersk said it is suspending all vessel crossings through the strait until further notice.

This is a supply disruption in real time, not a theoretical risk. And there is a bitter irony embedded in it: the conflict is materially improving Russia's competitive position in crude oil markets. With Middle East barrels facing logistical disruption, both India and China face strong incentives to deepen reliance on Russian supply.

## What South Africa and Africa Must Understand

For South Africa, this crisis arrives at a moment of acute vulnerability. For the South African government, the US/Israel attack on Iran and the death of Khamenei is more than just a violation of international law, as President Ramaphosa has called it — it is a potentially lethal blow to an ally. The United States has already imposed tariffs of up to 40 percent on certain South African exports. The relationship with Washington is at its lowest point in the democratic era.

Weeks before the strikes, Pretoria was already entangled in the Iran question. In January, three Iranian warships arrived at Simon's Town for BRICS naval drills. According to diplomatic sources, the Presidency maintained Ramaphosa had not authorised the participation of Iran. Ramaphosa ordered the withdrawal of the Iranian ships — but his orders were ignored. This likely highlights the absence of a shared strategic vision within BRICS and exposes South Africa's growing difficulty in navigating great-power politics. As BRICS expands and geopolitical polarisation sharpens, Pretoria's choices raise urgent questions about the meaning of non-alignment.

The wider African picture is equally stark. Iran's accession to BRICS has added complexity and may strain the bloc's unity. African members like South Africa, Egypt and Ethiopia could face awkward choices should the bloc lean towards favouring Tehran. There is an indirect security risk arising from reprioritisation of Western strategic interests. With defence budgets and attention stretched, Africa's security sectors may find themselves increasingly under-resourced. Africa often becomes an unintended theatre of competition and collateral. Nigeria, Cameroon, Chad, Mali, Burkina Faso and Niger are already feeling the brunt of Western retrenchment.

And then there is the oil price. South Africa imports virtually all of its crude. Every dollar added to the barrel price translates directly into higher fuel costs, higher food prices, and deeper pressure on an already strained consumer. If Brent reaches triple digits, as analysts now warn is plausible, the economic damage to import-dependent African economies will be severe and immediate.

## How to Read This Moment

The dominant narrative in the coming days will be about whether the operation "succeeds" — whether Iran's regime falls, whether the nuclear program is truly destroyed, whether a new government emerges. This is the framing that serves those who launched the war.

The more honest framing is this: the United States and Israel have assassinated the head of state of a sovereign country and decapitated its military command in strikes that the Pentagon itself admits were not provoked by an imminent attack. Two sources said the claim that Iran will soon have a missile capable of hitting the US is not backed up by intelligence. The nuclear talks were still active. Iran had agreed to halt enrichment. None of this mattered.

What mattered was the convergence of Netanyahu's electoral timeline, Trump's appetite for spectacle, and a shared strategic desire to reassert control over the Gulf at a moment when the old order is fracturing. Attention will now turn to the response of BRICS, particularly its African member states. These developments raise a broader question about BRICS itself: does it function as a cohesive grouping capable of coordinated political positioning, or is it primarily a loose alignment of states with divergent interests?

For South Africa and for every country in the Global South that has been told the "rules-based international order" protects them: this weekend was the answer. The rules apply only when they serve the powerful. When they do not, they are discarded — and the rest of the world is left to manage the consequences.
