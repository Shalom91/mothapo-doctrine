Yesterday, March 11, 2026, was the most consequential date in American AI regulation that almost nobody outside legal circles noticed. Two federal deliverables were due: the Commerce Department's evaluation identifying state AI laws it considers "onerous," and the FTC's policy statement on when state AI laws are preempted by federal consumer protection law. Together, these documents form the operational blueprint for the Trump administration's campaign to strip states of the ability to regulate artificial intelligence — at the precise moment AI systems are being deployed into clinical healthcare faster than anyone can validate them.

## What Happened

On December 11, 2025, President Trump signed an Executive Order titled "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence," declaring it the policy of the United States to achieve "global AI dominance through a minimally burdensome national policy framework for AI." The order's language was clear about its purpose: "To win, United States AI companies must be free to innovate without cumbersome regulation. But excessive State regulation thwarts this imperative."

The executive order itself does not repeal any state law. It does not itself preempt, repeal, or invalidate any state AI law. Instead, it directs a sequenced series of federal agency actions that could result in litigation challenging state AI laws, new agency guidance on the application of existing federal statutes, and conditions on federal funding.

The machinery it set in motion operates on three tracks. First, Attorney General Pam Bondi established the Department of Justice's AI Litigation Task Force on January 9, 2026, tasked with challenging state AI laws in federal court. Second, the Department of Commerce was tasked with conducting a nationwide review of state AI statutes and submitting its findings to the White House by March 11, 2026. Third, the Executive Order directs the Commerce Department to issue a Policy Notice conditioning remaining BEAD Program nondeployment funds — estimated at approximately $21 billion — on states not maintaining "onerous" AI laws.

The financial coercion here is worth pausing on. Members of Congress are already pressing the Commerce Department on which laws to target. Reps. Gabe Evans (R-Colo.) and Nick Langworthy (R-NY) wrote to Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick on Feb. 19 urging the department to include Colorado's AI Act and New York's RAISE Act in the evaluation. The mechanism is straightforward: states that pass AI safety laws risk losing billions in broadband infrastructure funding. This is not regulation. It is extortion dressed in administrative process.

## Who Asked for This

The dominant framing presents this as a sensible response to regulatory fragmentation — who wants to comply with 50 different state laws? But this framing omits the most important question: who lobbied for this outcome, and what do they gain?

In exchange for voluntary data sharing with the federal government, OpenAI requested the private sector receive "relief from the 781 and counting proposed AI-related bills already introduced this year in US states." The strategy embraced by OpenAI also involves invoking the "China threat" narrative, a powerful argument suggesting that stringent domestic regulations could impede the U.S.'s ability to compete against China in AI technology. The combined political spending of the tech sector on this issue surpassed $1.1 billion in 2024-2025.

Consider what the targeted state laws actually do. California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act requires developers of large AI models to publish risk frameworks, report critical safety incidents within 15 days, and implement whistleblower protections, with penalties up to $1 million per violation. Colorado's AI Act requires reasonable care to avoid algorithmic discrimination. California's Companion Chatbots Act mandates safety protocols against suicidal content in AI chatbots used by minors. These are not radical impositions. They are the bare minimum of accountability for systems that are already affecting millions of lives.

The executive order, notably, frames state laws designed to prevent algorithmic discrimination as requiring entities to "embed ideological bias within models," specifically citing Colorado's law as potentially forcing "AI models to produce false results." This is an extraordinary rhetorical move: redefining anti-discrimination requirements as compelled falsehood.

## The Legal Theory's Weakness

The underlying theory is that certain state laws requiring AI developers to adjust model outputs to mitigate bias could, in the administration's view, compel the production of outputs that are "deceptive" under federal law — a theory that commentators have described as untested.

The legal situation is genuinely uncertain. Though a policy statement could not create new preemptive effect, the administration seems to indicate the document would merely explain how the FTC Act already preempts certain state laws. Given the lack of specificity in the FTC Act's general prohibitions, and the clear congressional intent against preemption, the federal government would have an uphill battle to enjoin a state law based on such an interpretation. Congress itself has already rejected explicit preemption: on May 22, 2025, the U.S. House of Representatives passed a provision that would have barred states from enforcing AI-specific regulations for ten years. However, the Senate ultimately voted to remove this provision, leaving state enforcement authority intact.

What the executive order achieves, even if its legal theories fail, is deterrence. By raising the financial and legal costs and uncertainty associated with enacting and defending state AI laws, the Executive Order may create a deterrent effect that discourages state legislatures from pursuing new regulations, regardless of what the courts decide.

## Meanwhile, in Healthcare

The timing is remarkable. This week, while the federal government published its roadmap for dismantling state AI oversight, AI agents are proliferating in healthcare faster than they can be counted. Epic Systems, the nation's largest electronic health records vendor, touted three agents: "Art" for medical documentation, "Penny" for billing, and "Emmie" for answering patients' questions. Oracle has rolled out its own agent to help physicians in 30 specialties draft notes and suggest next steps for patients. Meanwhile, Amazon, Google, and Microsoft are all adding AI personas — part of a flood of new tools launched this week at HIMSS.

Experts raise concerns that products are not sufficiently tested with patients. These models are "probabilistic and predictive in nature, which introduces variability and potential inaccuracy into clinical documentation." "When AI-generated outputs are accepted without validation, unsupported diagnoses, incomplete clinical context and subtle inaccuracies can become part of the permanent record."

The Trump administration has largely moved to limit rules that could slow the adoption of the technology, leaving healthcare organizations with less guidance on how to implement AI. As one panellist at HIMSS put it bluntly: "At some point, some agent is going to make a mistake, and there will be a malpractice lawsuit, and the question is, who's liable?"

The core tension at HIMSS26 is familiar but getting worse. Healthcare organizations are deploying AI into clinical workflows, administrative operations, and patient-facing systems faster than they're building the structures to govern it.

## What This Actually Means

The story the mainstream frame tells is "federal government streamlines AI regulation." The actual story is this: the largest AI companies spent over a billion dollars lobbying for the removal of state-level safety requirements. They got an executive order that weaponises federal funding and litigation to achieve what Congress twice refused to legislate. The legal theory is untested and may well fail — but the chilling effect on state legislators is the point. And all of this is happening while AI agents are being deployed into healthcare at a pace that outstrips any validation framework, with the federal government explicitly declining to fill the regulatory vacuum it is creating.

For companies developing or deploying AI, the practical takeaway is that the regulatory environment is about to become more uncertain, not less. The honest framing for everyone else is simpler: the people building these systems have successfully lobbied to ensure that, for the foreseeable future, nobody with enforcement power is checking whether they work safely. The Executive Order itself cannot overturn existing state law — that can only be done by an act of Congress or the courts. Until the relevant legal challenges are resolved, state laws remain enforceable, and organizations must continue to comply. But the signal has been sent. And in healthcare, where the consequences of AI error are measured in human lives, that signal may prove to be the most dangerous output of all.
