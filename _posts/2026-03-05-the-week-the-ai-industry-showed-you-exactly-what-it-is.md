Last week delivered three apparently separate AI stories. First, OpenAI closed a $110 billion funding round — the largest private financing in history. Second, the Trump administration blacklisted Anthropic as a "supply chain risk to national security" after it refused to let the Pentagon use its AI without restrictions on mass surveillance and autonomous weapons, and OpenAI swooped in with its own Pentagon deal hours later. Third, the March 11 deadline approaches for the Commerce Department to publish its list of state AI laws deemed "onerous" — the prelude to federal lawsuits designed to kill state-level AI regulation.

These are not three stories. They are one story. It is a story about the rapid consolidation of artificial intelligence into a system where unprecedented capital, military power, and deregulation converge under a small number of actors — and where the mechanisms that might have provided public accountability are being systematically dismantled.

## The $110 Billion Question

OpenAI announced $110 billion in new investment at a $730 billion pre-money valuation. Amazon invested $50 billion, Nvidia invested $30 billion and SoftBank invested $30 billion in the round. This marks the largest private financing in history, surpassing the company's previous round, which closed in March 2025, raising $40 billion against a $300 billion valuation — itself the record at the time.

The mainstream framing treats this as a validation story: OpenAI is winning the AI race, investors believe in its vision, this is good for American competitiveness. What this framing omits is the financial structure underneath.

Internal OpenAI documents predict the company is set to lose $14 billion in 2026, and will continue to make huge losses totalling $44 billion until 2029. The company now expects to burn approximately $218 billion between 2026 and 2029, about $111 billion more than internal forecasts made just two quarters earlier. Its revenue projections — targeting about $280 billion in revenue in 2030 after generating $13.1 billion last year — would require growth at a pace for which there are no historical examples of companies growing at such a fast pace from such a high level.

The $110 billion is not a vote of confidence in profitability. It is a strategic bet by three companies — Amazon, Nvidia, SoftBank — each of which has its own reasons to keep the AI capital cycle spinning. Amazon's pursuit of a stake in OpenAI while maintaining heavy investments in Anthropic underscores a hedging strategy. By backing both leading developers, Amazon secures leverage over infrastructure demands, positioning AWS as indispensable regardless of which models dominate. Nvidia is investing in its own largest customer. SoftBank is doubling down on its AI-infrastructure play through Stargate. The money is not flowing toward OpenAI because the business case is proven. It is flowing because each investor needs OpenAI to keep spending.

As Axios put it, "there seems to be no end to the ChatGPT maker's need for cash." For context, venture capitalists invested a total of $170 billion into all U.S. startups in 2023. One company is now raising two-thirds of that in a single round.

## The Pentagon Play

Now place the funding story next to Friday's events. President Trump ordered the U.S. government to stop using Anthropic's products. Trump's announcement came about an hour before a deadline set by the Pentagon. Shortly after the deadline passed, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said he was labeling Anthropic a supply chain risk to national security.

The substance of the dispute was narrow. Anthropic wanted assurance that its models would not be used for fully autonomous weapons or mass surveillance of Americans, while the DoD wanted Anthropic to agree to let the military use the models across all lawful use cases. Anthropic argued — correctly — that the law today does not contemplate AI. The Pentagon refused. Anthropic feared that artificial intelligence could supercharge the collection and subsequent surveillance of Americans using geolocation, web browsing data, or personal financial information purchased from data brokers.

Hours after the Pentagon's announcement, OpenAI CEO Sam Altman said his company had agreed to terms with the DoD. After facing a barrage of criticism over the weekend, Altman followed up acknowledging his timing was "sloppy" and that the company "shouldn't have rushed" the deal. The language in OpenAI's contract is specifically about the "unconstrained" collection of Americans' private information — not public information that critics say can also lead to technically legal mass surveillance.

This matters because the distinction between "private" and "public" information is precisely the gap Anthropic identified. In the age of data brokers, your location data, browsing habits, and financial transactions are all "public" in the legal sense. AI makes it trivially cheap to aggregate these into comprehensive surveillance profiles. OpenAI's contract language — prohibiting "unconstrained" collection of "private" information — may sound protective. It is the appearance of a red line drawn around a loophole.

OpenAI employee Leo Gao criticized his employer for agreeing to let the DoW use its technology for "all lawful purposes" and then engaging in what Gao called "window dressing." Tara Chklovski, CEO of Technovation, said that if the Defense Department pursues this strategy to its end and cuts off Anthropic, it could be a dangerous decision — Anthropic has been the most deliberate model creator when it comes to building systems for the military, and any alternative supplier will be less safe.

The message to the entire AI industry is clear: comply with the Pentagon's terms, or face not just the loss of government contracts but active designation as a threat to national security. So far, in addition to the Defense Department, officials at the Treasury Department, State Department and Health and Human Services have directed employees to move off Claude.

## The Regulatory Kill Shot

Meanwhile, the executive order directs the Secretary of Commerce to publish, by March 11, 2026, a comprehensive review of existing state AI laws, identifying those deemed overly burdensome or in conflict with federal policy — particularly laws that address algorithmic discrimination, require transparency about AI decision-making, or mandate independent audits.

The mechanism is blunt. The executive order instructs the Department of Commerce to condition $42 billion in previously allocated broadband infrastructure funding on the repeal of state AI regulations deemed onerous. It uses federal funding as leverage to limit state AI regulation. The FTC has been directed to issue a policy statement classifying state-mandated bias mitigation as a per se deceptive trade practice. The legal theory: if an AI model is trained on data reflecting societal patterns, forcing developers to alter outputs to mitigate bias compels them to produce results that are less faithful to the underlying data — and therefore "deceptive."

Read that again. Under this theory, an AI system that reproduces historical discrimination is being "truthful." An AI system that attempts to correct for that discrimination is "deceptive." This is not a regulatory framework. It is a legal doctrine designed to make anti-discrimination law incompatible with AI deployment.

Thirty-six state attorneys general have voiced opposition, warning that a broad federal moratorium would freeze states' ability to respond as new risks emerge. The Supremacy Clause requires that only a law passed by Congress can preempt a contrary state law. The executive order does not directly preempt state AI laws; rather, it uses a method of withholding congressionally allocated funding to coerce states to repeal the AI laws the administration disagrees with.

Congress has so far been unwilling to take away most state authority to regulate AI. Advocates of state preemption, including Senate Commerce Chair Ted Cruz, fell short last year on attaching preemption to the GOP budget reconciliation law or the fiscal 2026 defense policy law. What couldn't pass through Congress is being imposed through executive coercion.

## Who Benefits from the Convergence

The three events share a common beneficiary: companies that want to deploy AI at maximum scale with minimum accountability. The $110 billion keeps the infrastructure buildout going. The Pentagon deal establishes a precedent that AI companies must accept government terms without meaningful safety conditions. The regulatory assault removes the last domestic constraint — state laws that require transparency, bias audits, or consumer protection.

Meanwhile, the actual labour market effects are mounting. In the first two months of 2026, there were 32,000 job losses in technology firms. Layoffs dominated the U.S. job market in 2025, with nearly 55,000 job cuts directly attributed to AI, out of a total 1.17 million layoffs. Unemployment among 20- to 30-year-olds in tech-exposed occupations has risen by almost 3 percentage points since the start of 2025, corroborating reports that generative AI is contributing to hiring headwinds facing recent college graduates. Deutsche Bank analysts warn that "AI redundancy washing will be a significant feature of 2026" — companies using AI as a scapegoat for layoffs driven by other factors.

The honest picture is this: the labour effects are real but uneven, the safety mechanisms are being removed faster than the risks are being understood, and the capital structure demands perpetual expansion regardless of whether the underlying economics justify it. The people making these decisions — a handful of CEOs, a small number of enormous investors, and an administration ideologically committed to deregulation — are not accountable to the workers being displaced, the citizens who might be surveilled, or the communities whose AI protections are being stripped.

## What This Means

When a company worth $730 billion is projected to lose $14 billion this year, depends on revenue growth that has no historical precedent, and is simultaneously signing classified military contracts and benefiting from the destruction of state-level regulation, you are not looking at a technology story. You are looking at a power story. The week's events revealed the architecture clearly: capital, military integration, and regulatory capture operating in concert.

The question is no longer whether AI will be transformative. It is who will decide the terms of that transformation — and whether anyone outside a very small room will have any say at all.
